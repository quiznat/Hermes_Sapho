## Core thesis
Professional developers approach AI coding agents with discipline rather than improvisation, prioritizing control mechanisms to preserve software quality. Their practices reflect hard-won engineering judgment: agents prove useful for well-defined tasks but require careful oversight and validation to deliver genuine productivity gains.

## Why it matters for Sapho
AI vendor narratives often promise frictionless augmentation, yet the ground truth among experienced practitioners tells a different story. Understanding how skilled developers actually constrain and direct agentic tools reveals the true contours of productivity gains—and where current technology falls short of its promises.

## Key findings
- **Quality over convenience.** Developers prioritize control mechanisms that protect software quality rather than accepting agent outputs uncritically.
- **Disciplined productivity.** Experienced practitioners achieve productivity gains through careful planning and validation, not by deferring to AI suggestions.
- **Scope boundaries matter.** Agents prove suitable for straightforward, well-described coding tasks; complex problems expose real limitations in current systems.
- **External evidence aligns.** Independent trials found AI access slowed maintainers by 19%, and an issue-tracking agent succeeded in only 8% of invocations—underscoring why professionals remain cautious.

## Limits
This synthesis draws from an excerpted study involving 13 field observation participants and 99 survey respondents; the partial material may omit methodological details, full data distributions, or additional caveats present in the complete paper.
