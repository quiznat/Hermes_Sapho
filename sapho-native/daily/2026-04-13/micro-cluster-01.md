## Cluster Thesis

Selective memory management appears to be a first-order systems choice, not a cleanup step after retrieval fails. This article argues that strong write-time gating, paired with archival retention and lineage for displaced knowledge, can sharply reduce distractor pressure while preserving access to prior states, making memory both more accurate and more historically accountable.

## Shared Signals

- Memory quality is shaped upstream by admission policy, not only downstream by retrieval ranking.
- A small active store can outperform a larger undifferentiated store when distractors are aggressively excluded.
- Archiving with lineage preserves temporal and superseded knowledge without letting stale material dominate current retrieval.
- For Sapho, the result supports stricter active-memory boundaries, explicit supersession, and fail-closed admission discipline.
